Indicators

This report compares two country-level development indicators for 2020:

  1. GDP per capita (international-$) — economic output per person, adjusted for international dollars.
  2. Child mortality (deaths per 1,000 live births, ages 0–5) — number of children who die before age five per 1,000 live births.

Each indicator is subset to the year 2020. The sample sizes are 193 countries with GDP data and 194 with child mortality data; 193 countries have both values and appear in the scatterplot below.

World map: GDP per capita (2020)

Higher GDP per capita (darker colors on a log scale) clusters in North America, Western Europe, Oceania, and parts of East Asia. Much of sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia show lower values.

World map: Child mortality (2020)

Child mortality is highest across much of sub-Saharan Africa and pockets of South Asia, and lowest in Europe, North America, East Asia, and Australia. The geographic pattern is broadly the inverse of the GDP map.

Interpretation

In 2020, GDP per capita and under-five child mortality show a strong negative association across countries (Pearson r = -0.45). Wealthier countries tend to have much lower child death rates; poorer countries bear the highest burden of child mortality.

The relationship is nonlinear: gains in GDP at low income levels coincide with especially large reductions in mortality, while among high-income countries both indicators level off (mortality approaches very low values). This pattern is visible on the log-scaled x-axis and matches the contrasting regional patterns in the two maps—regions with low GDP per capita in 2020 largely overlap those with elevated child mortality.

The association does not imply causation on its own—health systems, nutrition, conflict, and demographics also matter—but it highlights how economic resources and child survival outcomes remain tightly linked at the global scale in 2020.
